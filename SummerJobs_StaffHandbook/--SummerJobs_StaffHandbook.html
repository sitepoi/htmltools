Welcome & Program Overview

Welcome to the team! We are thrilled to have you join us through the Canada Summer Jobs (CSJ) program, a national initiative that creates meaningful summer work experiences for young Canadians aged 15–30.

What is Canada Summer Jobs?

Canada Summer Jobs connects young Canadians with employers to develop real job skills, build professional experience, and contribute positively to their organizations and communities. You are here to work, grow, and make a genuine contribution — and we are here to support that.

🎯 Program Goals

Section 2

Your Rights & Entitlements

As an employee in Canada, you are protected by federal and provincial/territorial employment standards legislation. Your rights cannot be waived or reduced below these minimums, regardless of any agreement to the contrary.

Core Employment Rights

  • Right to be paid at least the applicable minimum wage on time
  • Right to a safe and healthy workplace free from hazards
  • Right to be free from harassment and discrimination
  • Right to refuse unsafe work without fear of reprisal
  • Right to know your rights under the Canadian Human Rights Act and applicable provincial acts
  • Right to statutory holidays (or pay in lieu) as applicable
  • Right to a rest period — at least 30 minutes for every 5 consecutive hours worked
  • Right to receive a Record of Employment (ROE) at the end of your contract

Statutory Holidays During Your Term

Your employment term is a mandatory 8 weeks of full-time work at 35 hours per week (280 total paid hours). Statutory holidays that fall within your employment period are paid days off, but because the program requires the full 280 hours to be completed, any week containing a statutory holiday must be managed according to the policy below.

HolidayTypical DateEntitlement

Managing Holidays Within the 8-Week / 35 hrs/Week Requirement

Because the CSJ program mandates a full 280 paid working hours across the employment term, the following rules apply whenever a statutory holiday falls on a scheduled workday:

📅
Standard Approach — Contract Extension: For each statutory holiday that falls within your contract period, your contract end date is automatically extended by one (1) working day. This ensures the full 280 hours are completed. Your supervisor will confirm your adjusted end date at the start of your term based on holidays that fall within it.
  • You will be notified of your official start and end dates (including any extensions for holidays) at or before the start of your contract
  • The holiday itself is a fully paid day — you do not lose pay for observing it
  • In some cases, the supervisor may instead arrange a make-up schedule (e.g., slightly extended shifts on other days of the same week) — this requires written confirmation from your supervisor in advance
  • You may not skip a statutory holiday and work that day as a regular workday without explicit written supervisor approval
  • Attendance expectations — punctuality, conduct, and professionalism — remain fully in effect on all other days of a holiday week
ℹ️
Note: Specific holiday entitlements may vary by province or territory. Your supervisor will clarify which statutory holidays apply to your location and update your contract end date accordingly before your first day.

Break Period

  • Unauthorized work beyond scheduled hours will not be compensated and may result in disciplinary action

Remote/Hybrid Work

If your position has an approved remote or hybrid component, the same attendance and availability expectations apply. You must be reachable by phone or email during your scheduled hours and attend all required virtual meetings on time.

1. Orientation & Onboarding (Week 1)

Your first week is structured to fully integrate you into our organization. You will:

  • Tour the entire workplace and meet every member of your team and all supervisors

  • Complete all required administrative paperwork

2. Workplace Health & Safety Training

No staff member may perform physical or hands-on duties until OHS training is completed. This training covers:

  • Identifying, reporting, and controlling hazards in your specific work area
  • Proper use of all Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) assigned to your role
  • Safe operation of tools, equipment, and machinery you are authorized to use
  • Ergonomics — proper lifting, sitting, and movement techniques to prevent injury
  • WHMIS 2015 — identifying hazard symbols, reading Safety Data Sheets (SDS), safe handling of chemicals
  • Emergency evacuation procedures — routes, assembly points, roles during an emergency

3. Ethics & Professional Conduct Training

Ethics training is a core requirement for all staff. You are not just expected to know the rules — you are expected to understand why they exist and live by them every day. This training covers:

  • Workplace integrity: Acting honestly and transparently in all tasks, communications, and decisions
  • Conflict of interest: Recognizing situations where personal interests may conflict with your duties and disclosing them promptly
  • Accountability: Taking full ownership of your actions, your mistakes, and their consequences — never shifting blame
  • Ethical decision-making: A practical framework for identifying and resolving ethical dilemmas you may face on the job
  • Reporting unethical behaviour: You have a professional and moral obligation to speak up when you witness wrongdoing, waste, or dishonesty
  • Confidentiality as an ethical duty: Understanding why protecting private information is not just a policy requirement, but a matter of professional integrity
  • Fair treatment: Applying consistent, unbiased standards in all interactions with colleagues, supervisors, and clients

4. Moral Principles & Integrity Training

Beyond policy compliance, we expect every staff member to operate with a genuine moral compass. This is a core part of working here — not a formality. This training addresses the personal character qualities we hold to the highest standard:

Core Moral Principles

  • Honesty: Tell the truth always — even when it is difficult or uncomfortable
  • Respect: Treat every person with full dignity regardless of role, background, or status
  • Responsibility: Do what you committed to. Do not make excuses.
  • Compassion: Be aware of how your words and actions affect others

Professional Character

  • Fairness: Apply the same standard to everyone — no favouritism
  • Courage: Speak up against wrongdoing even when it is uncomfortable
  • Trustworthiness: Be someone colleagues, supervisors, and clients can rely on fully
  • Humility: Receive feedback openly and always be willing to learn

Section 5

Workplace Health & Safety

🚨
Priority One: Your health and safety is our most important concern. Every employee — regardless of their role — has the right to a safe workplace and the right to refuse unsafe work.

Legislative Framework

Workplace safety in Canada is governed by:

  • Provincial/territorial Occupational Health and Safety (OHS) Acts
  • The Canada Labour Code (for federally regulated workplaces)
  • WHMIS 2015 – Workplace Hazardous Materials Information System

Three Core Rights of Every Worker

1️⃣ Right to Know

You have the right to know about any hazards in your workplace, including chemicals, equipment, and procedures that may pose a risk to your health or safety.

2️⃣ Right to Participate

You have the right to be involved in health and safety decisions, raise concerns, and participate in workplace safety committees or discussions.

3️⃣ Right to Refuse Unsafe Work

You have the right to refuse work that you reasonably believe is dangerous to yourself or others. You must immediately report the concern to your supervisor. You will not be penalized for exercising this right.

General Safety Rules — All Staff Must Follow

  • Complete all required safety orientations and training before starting work
  • Always use Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) as required for your role (gloves, safety vest, eye protection, etc.)
  • Report all hazards, near misses, injuries, and accidents to your supervisor immediately
  • Never operate equipment you have not been trained to use
  • Keep your work area clean, organized, and free of slip/trip hazards
  • Do not bring or consume alcohol or drugs on work premises
  • Know the locations of: fire extinguishers, first aid kits, emergency exits, and eyewash stations
  • Follow all posted signs, warnings, and safety notices
  • Do not use your phone while operating equipment or performing safety-sensitive tasks

If an Accident or Injury Occurs

  1. Ensure immediate safety — move yourself and others away from danger if possible
  2. Call for help or call 911 if it is a medical emergency
  3. Notify your supervisor immediately (same day)
  4. Complete an Incident/Accident Report form — available from your supervisor
  5. Seek first aid or medical attention if required
  6. Cooperate fully with any investigation
📋
WHMIS Training: If your role involves handling any chemicals, cleaning products, or hazardous materials, you must complete WHMIS training before handling those substances. Ask your supervisor if you are unsure.

Mental Health & Wellness

We recognize that well-being includes mental health. If you are experiencing stress, anxiety, or personal difficulties that are affecting your work:

  • Speak confidentially with your supervisor or HR contact
  • Use the free Crisis Line: 1-833-456-4566 (Canada Suicide Prevention Service – 24/7)
  • Access Kids Help Phone (if under 25): 1-800-668-6868 or text HELLO to 686868
  • We have a zero-tolerance policy for workplace bullying that affects mental wellness

Workplace Conduct & Professionalism

We expect all staff to conduct themselves in a professional, respectful, and responsible manner at all times — whether you are in the office, at an event, working remotely, or representing the organization in the community.

Standards of Conduct

  • Treat all colleagues, supervisors, clients, and members of the public with dignity and respect
  • Communicate professionally — in person, by phone, email, or chat
  • Be cooperative, flexible, and willing to take on assigned tasks
  • Maintain a positive attitude and contribute to a healthy team environment
  • Ask for help when you are unsure — it is always better to ask than to guess

Unacceptable Conduct — Zero Tolerance

Section 7

Harassment, Discrimination & Equity

Our organization is committed to providing a workplace that is free from harassment and discrimination. We uphold the principles of equity, diversity, and inclusion in everything we do.

Protected Grounds Under the Canadian Human Rights Act

No person shall be discriminated against based on:

  • Race or colour
  • National or ethnic origin
  • Religion or faith
  • Age
  • Sex / Gender
  • Sexual orientation
  • Gender identity or expression
  • Marital or family status
  • Disability (physical or mental)
  • Genetic characteristics
  • Pardoned conviction
  • Pregnancy or breastfeeding
  • Indigenous identity

What Constitutes Harassment?

Harassment includes, but is not limited to:

  • Verbal abuse, insults, put-downs, or mockery
  • Offensive jokes, comments, or gestures related to a protected ground
  • Unwanted physical contact
  • Sexual harassment — unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favours, or sexual comments
  • Cyberbullying or online harassment using organizational devices or accounts
  • Deliberate exclusion or social isolation of a colleague
  • Displaying or sharing offensive images, materials, or content

How to Report

📣
You are encouraged and protected when you report. No employee will face retaliation for making a good-faith harassment or discrimination complaint.
  1. Document the incident: date, time, location, what was said or done, witnesses
  2. Report to your direct supervisor or, if they are the subject of the complaint, to HR or the next level of management
  3. A confidential investigation will be initiated within 5 business days
  4. Both parties will be notified of the outcome and any corrective action taken

Complaints can also be filed externally with the Canadian Human Rights Commission (CHRC) at chrc-ccdp.gc.ca or your provincial Human Rights Tribunal.

Section 12

Emergency Procedures

📞
Emergency Services: Call 911 immediately for any life-threatening emergency. Do not hesitate.

Fire Emergency

  1. Activate the nearest fire alarm pull station
  2. Call 911 if the fire is beyond a very early stage
  3. Evacuate the building using the nearest safe exit — do NOT use elevators
  4. Proceed to the designated Assembly Point (confirm location with supervisor on Day 1)
  5. Do not re-enter the building until authorized by emergency responders
  6. Assist persons with disabilities to the nearest stairwell refuge area if evacuation is not possible

Medical Emergency

  1. Call 911 immediately if the person is unresponsive, not breathing, or in serious distress
  2. Send a colleague to meet paramedics at the building entrance
  3. Retrieve the first aid kit — location: _____________________ (ask supervisor)
  4. Only administer first aid if you are trained to do so
  5. Notify your supervisor immediately
  6. Complete an Incident Report form

Lockdown / Security Threat

  1. If you see or suspect a security threat, call 911 immediately
  2. Follow the Run – Hide – Fight framework:
    • Run: Evacuate if you can do so safely
    • Hide: Lock or barricade yourself in a room, silence your phone
    • Fight: As an absolute last resort, defend yourself
  3. Do not open doors for anyone until given the all-clear by authorities
  4. Do not post about the situation on social media during the incident
